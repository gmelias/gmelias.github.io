Chemistry of Life

Pre-Class Learning Goals

  • Describe the structure of an atom
    • The atom has protons, neutrons and electrons.
  • Define and relate the terms electronegativity and polarity
    • Electronegativity is defined as how much atoms attract each other
    • Polarity is defined as how the electrons are shared within a molecule. A non-polar molecule has a symmetric appearance. This is because all the atoms have the same (or same-ish) electronegativity level. A polar molecule (like H2O) has an uneven appearance. This is because the atoms in the molcule have different levels of electronegativity.
  • Explain how nonpolar and polar covalent bonds are formed between atoms
    • Refer to the previous learning outcome
  • Explain how the potential energy of two atoms changes as they form or break a bond
    • Recall the Relative Potential Energy / Internuclear Distance Graph!
    • The lowest potential energy occurs when the atoms are the happiest. The stronger the bond, the further down the graph sweeps.
      • (To see bond strengths and how they relate, view the 2nd to last learning outcome in this section)
  • Describe each type of chemical bond and intermolecular force: nonpolar covalent bonds, polar covalent bonds, ionic bonds, hydrogen bonds, and van der Waals’ forces
    • Covalent bonds are the strongest (since we are thinking about ionic bonds in H2O), and they cannot occur with a metal
    • Ionic bonds are the 2nd strongest bonds, and they occur between any metal and non-metal
    • Hydrogen bonds are the 3rd strongest. This is the bond that occurs between ANY polar molecules. Since polar molecules tend to have hydrogen, this is the name.
    • van der Waals' forces are the weakest of the four. They occur to the random movement of electrons and can occur to anything/anywhere.
  • Define the terms “hydrophilic” and “hydrophobic"
    • Good to know: Polar bonds are generally hydrophilic, and non-polar bonds are generally hydrophobic.

    • H forms 1 covalent bond
    • O forms 2 covalent bond
    • N forms 3 covalent bond
    • C forms 4 covalent bonds
  • Compare and contrast the relative electronegativities of H, C, O, and N PLEASE CHECK THIS INFO
    • H and C have lower electronegativity
    • O and N have higher electronegativity

    • I normally don't codone memorization, but this seems like a good time to memorize the electronegativities from the previous learning outcome and use it. For example, if H and O are bonded, then it must be polar because of the uneven electronegativity and therefore unequal bond sharing.
  • Relate the potential energy change of an atomic interaction to its “strength”
    • Refer to the question about bond strength. Covalent bonds have the highest bond strength, and the lowest potential energy (because it would take a lot of energy to take them out of their 'happy place.' The highest potential energy would then be van der Waals because they are the weakest of the four bonds.
  • Compare the relative strengths of bonds and electrostatic interactions and relate these strengths to the amount of energy required to break them
    • As seen by the graph, the stronger the bond is, the lower its potential energy is.
    • This is because it'll take more energy to break it, which is equal to the distance between the min and the x-axis.

    • A molecule can only participate in hydrogen bonding if it is hydrophilic and therefore must be polar

  • Determine whether a molecule is likely to be hydrophilic or hydrophobic based on its ability to form hydrogen bonds with water
    • As long as the molecule is polar and has hydrogen, then it's hydrophilic. Otherwise, assume hydrophobic.

Cell Organization

Pre-Class Learning Goals

  • Compare and contrast the organization of prokaryotic/eukaryotic cells and plant/animal cells
    • Prokaryotic cellsdo NOT have a nucleus
      • The genetic material is free floating in the cell
      • DNA is in the nucleoid, which is a discrete region
      • In bacteria, this is a plasmid, or a circular ring of genetic material
      • Prokaryotic Cells are considerably smaller than eukaryotic cells
    • Eukaryotic cellshave a nucleus
      • Includes all plant, animal, fungi, etc cells
      • The nucleusallows for transcription and translation, and more advanced processes
      • Inside, there are other membrane-bound organelles, so there are specialized compartments for different functions.
    • Animal cells versus plant cells
      • Plant cells have chloroplasts (for sun energy).
      • Plants also have cell wall and do not use a mitochondra
  • Define the terms organelle, cytoplasm, and cytosol
    • Organelle is a compartment inside a eukaryotic cell which is membrane bound.
    • Cytoplasm is technically defined as the contents in the cell that are not the nucleus.
    • Cytosol is technically defined as the jelly environment that the nucleus and organelles live in.
    • However, BOTH cytoplasm and cytosol just mean the jelly substance in this course!
  • Describe the function of common eukaryotic organelles: mitochondrion, chloroplast, nucleus, endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi apparatus, lysosome, and vacuole
  • Identify the structures of different lipids: triglycerides, fatty acids, phospholipids, and cholesterol
  • Differentiate between saturated and unsaturated fatty acids
  • Describe the structure of the plasma membrane and the different ways that proteins can be associated with the lipid bilayer
  • Define and term diffusion
  • Explain how the chemical properties of a phospholipid bilayer allow it to act as a semipermeable membrane
  • Define and relate the terms tonicity, osmosis, hypertonic, hypotonic, and isotonic
  • Compare and contrast the different passive (simple diffusion, facilitated diffusion) and active (primary, secondary) transport mechanisms
